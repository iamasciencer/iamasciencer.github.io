Chemically Peculiar stars on the Pre-main sequence

In this paper, we try to contribute to a poorly researched topic: Are there Chemically Peculiar stars that are still in the process of being born?

We take one of the latest catalogues of Open Clusters and identified possible pre-main sequence CP stars using a few key methods:

  • Δ a photometry and spectral classification to identify CP stars
  • The fitting of spectral energy distributions and emission lines (Hα) to identify PMS stars

OUt of 45 original candidates, we find out that nine sources are likely PMS CP stars. One of the stars is also a newly detected CP candidate.

Apparent non-variable stars from the Kepler mission

We wanted to know if the stars that were flagged as "constant" in the database of the Kepler mission are really constant or if there are variable stars hidden in the data.

We analysed approxomately 138 500 stars via a Lomb-Scargle periodogram in three frequency domains:

  1. Below 0.1 c/d
  2. Between 0.1 - 2 c/d
  3. Between 2-25 c/d

to identify variability in these three frequency domains.

Out of all the investigated targets, 14 154 stars fulfill our criteria for non-variability.

Identifying Ae/Be stars in Gaia low-resolution BP/RP spectra

Using a similar method as with the Δ a photometry, we took a sample of known emission line stars and used two additional Hα-filters to create an index:

\(H_{\alpha} = \frac{H_{\alpha, \text{wide}}}{H_{\alpha, \text{narrow}}}\)

In this case study that "invented" this method, we clearly saw that (almost) every emission line star had \(H_{\alpha} < 3.1\).

This result will help in detect emission lines, especially hydrogen emission in numerous other stars.
